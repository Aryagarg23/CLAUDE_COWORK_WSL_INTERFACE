# Arya's writing voice

Canonical: `~/projects/AryaGarg23_Website/documentation/writing-style/VOICE.md` (read
it for the full laws, before→after examples, and reading list; `feedback-log.md` next
to it is the verbatim rejection record). Agent-memory pointer also at
`~/.claude/projects/-home-arya-projects/memory/arya-writing-voice.md`.

**One paragraph:** a person explaining something he actually did, in the plainest
sentences that still carry it. Dry, first person, slightly under-claimed. What he saw
is stated flat; what it might mean is offered as a hunch. Confidence lives in the
build, not the prose. He'd rather sound like a kid down a rabbit hole than an analyst
issuing verdicts, and treats every quotable sentence as a defect.

## The laws (distilled)

1. **Flat is confident** — facts stated plainly; loud reads as insecure.
2. **Certainty is scoped** — observations flat ("the tasks that broke it went against
   the model's defaults"); generalizations hedged ("my guess is", "seems to"). Never
   swapped: hedging an observation reads as self-doubt; flattening a generalization is
   slop. Scope caveats (n=5, one model) are confidence, not doubt.
3. **Mechanism, not philosophy** — what triggers what, what gets written where, real
   commands, a worked example. Why-reasoning attaches to a concrete mechanism or goes.
4. **A kid exploring, not a prophet** — theses are personal experiments; no industry
   diagnoses or civilizational verdicts. A hedge word doesn't rescue a grand sentence —
   the shape itself is the slop.
5. **Length tracks complexity** — over-explaining a simple thing reads weak.
6. **First person owns the risk** — "I wrote that risk into the spec."
7. **The honest word over the professional word** — even in formal slots; keep the
   slightly awkward honest version.
8. **Point at a pattern, don't inflate it** — name a small finding once, plainly.

## The slop list (all rejected by Arya verbatim — never)

Em-dash punchlines (near-zero em-dashes overall) · triadic slogans · aphoristic
closers · "X, not Y" antithesis · "X on purpose" · colon-led explainer openers ("How
it works:") · arrow chains in prose · absolutes about the future / "the industry" ·
corpo vocabulary (comprehensive, seamless, robust, leverage) · self-doubt hedges on
observations · unformatted identifiers mid-prose (backtick `recordOutcome()`).

**Master test: when a sentence sounds quotable, rewrite it until it sounds said.**

## Registers

- **Raw voice** (chat/Discord/his commits): lowercase, dropped apostrophes, slang,
  typos. Signal about the person, NOT the publication target — don't imitate.
- **Published voice**: the raw voice cleaned, not replaced ("Fuck it, we ball" →
  "the things that should have stopped me mostly haven't"). Short abrupt rhythm and
  willing fragments survive; slang survives only when one word does real work.
- **Site firepole layers**: white = plain honest story, no numbers/mechanism · blue =
  the evidence (commands, tables, first person, never datasheet voice) · red =
  personal stakes, honest not confessional.

North star: the Dynamicland FAQ — take Victor's floor (concreteness, declarative calm,
generosity), reject his ceiling (institutional "we", prophecy). Closest living model:
Julia Evans. Also: Simon Willison (blue), Andy Matuschak (epistemics), Robin Sloan,
Michael Nielsen, Craig Mod (red).

## Litmus before publishing

Sounds said, not written? · Any LinkedIn pull-quote sentence → rewrite · Confidence on
observations, humility on generalizations, never swapped? · Mechanism visible? ·
Delete each qualifier/flourish: meaning survives → it was padding · His raw-voice
version would say it shorter — usually yes.
